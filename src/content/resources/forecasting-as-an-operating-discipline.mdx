## A forecast is a practice, not a guess

It is tempting to treat a forecast as a single number to be hit or missed. More usefully, a forecast is a discipline: a regular, structured way of asking what the current operational signals imply about where results are heading.

Seen this way, the value is not in being exactly right. It is in the attention the practice requires, and in noticing, early, when reality begins to diverge from expectation.

## Forecasting from operational signals

Useful forecasting starts upstream of the financials, with the operational indicators that tend to move first.

- Demand and capacity drifting out of balance
- Quality or rework trending in one direction
- Commitments accumulating faster than they are cleared

These signals rarely give a precise figure. They give direction and timing, enough to prepare a response while there is still time to shape it.

> A forecast earns its value in the moment it is wrong and you notice quickly.

## Holding the forecast lightly

Because conditions change, a forecast is most useful when it is revisited often and held lightly. The discipline is in the cadence: looking again, comparing signal to outcome, and adjusting awareness as the operational picture moves.

Predictive operational intelligence is not certainty about the future. It is a steady practice of seeing emerging risk and opportunity a little earlier than the financial statements would reveal them.
